This is a list of all 240 essentially distinct solutions to the Soma cube
puzzle. Each solution is presented as a 3x3x3 array of the letters
W, Y, G, O, L, R, B, corresponding to the piece names as they're given
in the book Winning Ways by Berlekamp, Conway, and Guy. The solutions
are "canonical" in the sense that they always have piece G in the same
position; furthermore, piece B doesn't touch the "top" face.

Solutions also appear with the code name assigned to them by Berlekamp,
Conway, and Guy's "Somap". This code name consists of a letter representing
the "deficient" piece; a letter representing the "central" piece;
a number representing the "dexterity"; and a letter to distinguish
between solutions that have the same deficiency/centrality/dexterity.

(Mirror-image reflections are not considered different. Thus the code names
BB0 and BB7 are equivalent; also LL0 and RR7, LR0 and RL7, OO0 and OO3,
WL0 and WL6, WW0 and WW6, YL0 and YR7, as explained in the book.)

Each solution is also preceded by its serial number, based on a complete list
of all 11520 solutions that was produced by the dancing links algorithm.

The 11520 solutions consist of 240 sets of 48 solutions that are
equivalent under symmetries of the cube. Every such set contains exactly
one canonical solution. That complete list has the form
  WOG:WWG:YYG|ROO:LLG:YLB|RRO:LRB:YBB #1
  WOG:WWG:YYG|LOO:LRG:YRB|LLO:RRB:YBB #2
  WOG:WWG:YYG|LOO:LBG:YRR|LLO:BBR:YBR #3
     . . . 
  BBL:BRW:ORW|BLL:OLW:ORR|YYY:OGY:GGG #11519
  BBL:BRW:ORW|BLL:OLW:ORR|GGG:OGY:YYY #11520
and it lists solutions in the (somewhat arbitrary) order of discovery.

It turns out that
  GGG:WGL:WLL|YYY:WRL:RRB|YOO:OOB:RBB #3997
is its first solution that has piece G in the canonical place. That solution
isn't canonical, however, because it has B's in third position. The 4004th
solution, namely
  GGG:WGR:WRR|OOY:WOO:BRL|YYY:BLL:BBL #4004
is the first to be fully canonical; so it is the first solution, in the
list of 240 that appears below. That solution is shown with its code
name "OO3a" (because O is deficient and central, while W and Y are in
"dexter" position, according to the rules of Winning Ways; also because
the makers of the Somap arbitrarily called it solution "a" of type OO3).

The Somap in the book, on pages 911--912, actually shows the mirror image
of this solution, which has dexterity 0 instead of 3. Thus you can find
it under the name "O0a" --- at the top of a little pentagon in the
upper right corner. (The book abbreviates "OO" to "O".)

According to the listing below, solution #4004 leads to several
other solutions by changing just two or three pieces. For example, it
leads to solution #4390 by changing the positions of pieces W and G.
It leads to #4384 by changing W, G, and O. And so on. Every
neighboring solution that is obtainable by changing at most three
pieces is listed. (The book shows only a dotted link from O0a to O2q;
O2q is called "#4251 OO2q" below.)

A two-digit code is given after the "delta" letters of neighboring
solutions. This code specifies the rotation and/or reflection that will
put the altered solution back into canonical form. (The first digit
is between 0 and 5; it specifies a permutation of the coordinates.
The second digit is between 0 and 7; it specifies optional
complementation of the coordinates. Thus there are 48 possible
transformations; in mathematical jargon, these transformations form the
octahedral group O_3, the automorphisms of a three-dimensional cube.)

Notice, for example, that #4004 yields #4251 in two ways,
(i) by changing Y, G, and O, then applying transformation 55;
(ii) by changing W, L, and R, then applying transformation 00.
Transformation 00 is the identity transformation; in case (ii), the
changed solution is already in canonical form, because neither
G nor B has been moved.

The Somap is available online at
  http://www-cs-faculty.stanford.edu/~knuth/sgb.html
as the demonstration graphs "somap2.gb" and "somap3.gb". Graph
somap2 lists adjacencies only when just two pieces change position;
its vertices are the same as those of somap3, but it has only 616 arcs
instead of 2194. Graph somap3 includes all the adjacencies listed below. 

Strictly speaking, these graphs are "multigraphs", because they
have repeated edges: somap2 has two edges between #4329 and #4348;
somap3 has fourteen instances of repeated edges. [The Somap by
Berlekamp, Conway, and Guy reflects this duplicity by including
two versions of #4348, called W2f and W4f; thus their map actually
has 281 vertices!]

Two other demonstration graphs, "somapx2.gb" and "somapx3.gb", are
also available in the same place. These graphs have 480 vertices,
because they consider solutions to be equivalent only under
rotation, not reflection. They have no repeated edges.

All four graphs are in standard SGB format. The first two come with
three utility fields preset in each vertex, besides the vertex name:
   z.S is the solution name (e.g. "GGG:WGR:WRR|OOY:WOO:BRL|YYY:BLL:BBL")
   y.S is the code name, exclusive of the final letter (e.g. "OO3")
   x.I is the ASCII code of the letter (e.g. 'a', or 97)
The vertex name is its serial number (e.g. "#4004").
The other two (somapx2 and somapx3) don't include an x.I field.

The arcs of all four graphs have two utility fields preset, in addition
to their tip and length fields:
   a.I is the automorphism in hexadecimal notation (e.g. 0x32)
   b.S is the string of changed pieces (e.g. "WG")
Each length field is 2 or 3, depending on the number of pieces moved.
